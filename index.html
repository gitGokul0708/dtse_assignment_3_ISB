What is a Prediction Market?

A prediction market is an exchange-traded market created for the purpose of trading contracts whose value is dependent on the outcome of an event. For example, a contract might pay out $1.00 if a specific political candidate wins an election and $0.00 if they lose.

The Mechanics of Trading

1. Event Definition

An event with uncertain outcomes is defined. Each outcome gets a contract (often binary: Yes/No).

  • “Will Kamala Harris win the 2024 U.S. Presidential Election?”
  • “Will Apple release an AI-powered iPhone by June 2025?”
  • “Will global temperatures exceed 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels in 2024?”

2. Trading Contracts

Participants buy or sell shares tied to outcomes: A “Yes” share pays out $1 if the event happens, $0 if not. A “No” share pays $1 if it doesn’t happen, $0 if it does. Prices range from $0.00 to $1.00.

For example, if “Will SpaceX land Starship on Mars by 2030?” is trading at $0.22 for “Yes”, the market believes there’s a 22% chance it will happen.

3. Price = Probability

Market prices reflect aggregated beliefs. A price of $0.85 means there's an 85% chance the event will occur. A price of $0.05 suggests a 5% chance, meaning the market thinks it’s very unlikely. This is similar to how stock prices reflect collective expectations about a company’s value.

4. Settlement

After the event occurs (or deadline passes), contracts pay out. “Yes” holders get $1 per share if the outcome is true. “No” holders get $1 per share if the outcome is false. All other contracts expire worthless. Profits/losses are calculated as follows: (Payout - Purchase Price) multiplied by the number of shares.

Example Transactions

Example 1: The Political Election

You believe Candidate A will win an election. The shares for "Candidate A wins" are currently trading at $0.40. You buy 100 shares for a total investment of $40.

Scenario A: Candidate A wins

Your 100 shares are each redeemed for $1.00. You receive $100 back, making a profit of $60.

Scenario B: Candidate A loses

Your 100 shares expire worthless. You lose your initial investment of $40.

Example 2: The Box Office Hit

A new movie is predicted to be a flop, and shares for "Movie A grosses over $100M" are trading at just $0.15. You are confident it will be a hit and buy 500 shares for $75.

Scenario A: Movie A hits $100M

Your 500 shares are each redeemed for $1.00. You receive $500 back, making a profit of $425.

Scenario B: Movie A fails to hit $100M

Your 500 shares expire worthless. You lose your initial investment of $75.
